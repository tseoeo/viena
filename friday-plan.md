## Walking to Belvedere

From Blütengasse 14–16: south on Strohgasse → cross Rennweg → continue south on Reisnerstraße → right on Prinz-Eugen-Straße → main entrance at **Prinz-Eugen-Straße 27**. About 10–12 min slowly. Mostly flat, paved.

**Important**: enter via the **Upper Belvedere south entrance** (Prinz-Eugen-Straße). Do NOT enter via Lower Belvedere on Rennweg and try to walk up through the gardens — that slope is the mobility issue the research flagged.

## Upper Belvedere visit (~2 hours)

Three levels with elevator: ground floor, *Beletage* (1st floor), 2nd floor. **Start with the Beletage while energy is fresh** — the headline rooms are there.

**Must-see sequence on the Beletage:**

1. **Marble Hall** — the grand central reception room with frescoed ceiling and the postcard view down the gardens to St. Stephen's spire. Also the room where the **Austrian State Treaty was signed in 1955** (the moment Austria became fully independent again after WWII occupation).
2. **The Klimt rooms**:
   - ***The Kiss*** (1907–1908) — the gold-leaf icon. Smaller than expected; tends to draw a crowd of phone-photographers. Just stand and look.
   - ***Judith I*** (1901) — Klimt's other gold masterpiece. More intense than *The Kiss*. Worth a long pause.
3. **The Schiele rooms** — Klimt's protégé. Look for *Death and the Maiden* and *The Family* if displayed.
4. **Vienna 1900 / Secession material** — Kokoschka, Moser, the Klimt circle.

Then ground floor for medieval and baroque Austrian art, second floor for 19th-century Austrian art (Biedermeier, Hans Makart) — **skip these freely** if mom is flagging. The Klimt and the Marble Hall are the trip-justifying pieces.

**Practical:**
- Cloakroom near the entrance — leave coats, take only what you need
- Audio guides at the entrance (no Bulgarian — Ivan provides context as you go)
- Benches in the galleries
- Small café inside Upper Belvedere for a sit-down break before continuing
- The view from the Marble Hall windows is itself a stop — don't rush past it

## Belvedere garden walkthrough → Botanical Garden

After Upper Belvedere, exit out the back onto the upper terrace. Pause for the postcard view down the gardens.

Walk down the formal terraces — fountains, sphinxes, statuary. Stairs at points but ramped paths alongside; "some steps" is fine. Don't rush — this is half the experience.

About halfway down (or near the Lower Belvedere level), head **east** toward the **Alpengarten** (the small alpine garden that's part of the Belvedere complex). From the Alpengarten there's a connecting gate into the **Botanical Garden** of the University of Vienna.

**Confirm the gate is open** at the Upper Belvedere ticket desk when you arrive (it usually is during open hours, but worth a 30-second check).

**If the gate is closed for any reason**: exit on Rennweg, walk ~3 min east to the Botanical Garden's main entrance at **Mechelgasse 2**.

## Botanical Garden (~45 min)

Free entry, no ticket. Open from 10:00 to dusk. Flat, paved paths, plenty of benches.

In early May, look for: late tulips, magnolias (possibly past peak), peonies starting, alpine wildflowers, mature trees in fresh leaf, Japanese cherry varieties if any are still blooming.

Don't try to "do it all" — pick a slow loop, sit a few times, leave when satisfied. Exit on **Rennweg** for tram 71 to lunch.

## Lunch — primary and backup

**Primary: Meissl & Schadn**
- Address: Schubertring 10–12, 1010 Vienna (in the Grand Ferdinand Hotel)
- Open: daily 11:30–22:00
- **Booking app**: Quandoo (app or quandoo.at) — real-time availability, can book same-day if slots are open. Their own site (meisslundschadn.at) also has online booking. **Ideal**: book the night before. **Acceptable**: book Friday morning from the Botanical Garden via Quandoo if a 12:45 or 13:00 slot is showing.
- Why: destination Wiener Schnitzel restaurant. Veal (real Wiener Schnitzel is only veal). Open salon kitchen — watch the Schnitzel pounded and fried tableside. Refined but not stuffy. Michelin Bib Gourmand. Calm enough for an elderly diner.
- **Order**: *Frittatensuppe* to start (clear beef broth with sliced pancake-strips), *Wiener Schnitzel* with *Erdäpfel-Vogerlsalat* (potato-and-lamb's-lettuce salad), *Kaiserschmarrn* with stewed plums to finish, glass of Grüner Veltliner.

**Option B: Plachuttas Gasthaus zur Oper**
- Address: Walfischgasse 5/7, 1010 Vienna (right by the Vienna State Opera)
- Open: daily; reservation recommended via plachutta-oper.at
- Why: voted Vienna's most popular Wiener Schnitzel three years running. Veal, classical preparation. Traditional Gasthaus atmosphere — more "old Vienna inn" than Meissl & Schadn's hotel-restaurant feel. Equivalent quality tier.
- **Use this if**: Meissl & Schadn is full on May 1, or if mom prefers a more traditional inn atmosphere.

## Afternoon — Option A or Option B (decide on the day)

After lunch (~14:30), depending on energy, pick one.

**Option A — Karlskirche + Resselpark (lighter, ~45 min)**

Karlskirche is a 5-minute walk south of Meissl & Schadn (or 8 min from Plachuttas). Vienna's other postcard baroque dome — giant green copper dome, two enormous Roman-style spiral columns flanking the entrance, the most photographed church after Stephansdom.

- Step inside. Entry policies vary; sometimes the back is free for prayer, full ticket (~€8) gets you the panoramic lift inside the dome up to the frescoes. The lift is a bit much for tired parents — **just pay basic entry, sit on a pew, look up at the dome from below**.
- Then sit at the **Resselpark** reflecting pool in front for fifteen minutes. Calm crowd, fountains, a Henry Moore sculpture.
- Tram or U4 back to apartment.

This adds Vienna's other major baroque sight at almost zero mobility cost.

**Option B — Stadtpark + Kursalon (slightly more, ~75 min, leans on the concert)**

Tram from Karlsplatz/Oper area to Stadtpark, walk slowly through the park, find the **gold Johann Strauss statue** in the gilded archway — Vienna's most photographed statue.

Sit at the **Kursalon café terrace** for a Melange and a slice of Sachertorte. The Kursalon is where Strauss conducted *On the Beautiful Blue Danube* premieres in the 1860s — a poetic close to the trip's musical arc with Thursday's symphony concert.

Then ~5 min walk home through the park.

This option is calm but requires more walking than A.

**How to choose on the day**: A if mom is tired or weather is poor (Karlskirche is indoors); B if she's energized and wants the concert-connection moment in fresh air.

## Evening

After Option A or B, slow walk back to apartment, rest.
